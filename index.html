Team Transportation Transformers · Las Positas College · Livermore, CA

Turn the campus into a solar power plant that funds clean transportation.

Transportation is the single largest source of Las Positas' carbon emissions. The plan: roughly double our solar to about 4.35 MW by adding carport canopies over the parking lots, free up the ~$1M/year we pay PG&E, and reinvest the profit into free EV charging and grants that help students and staff buy a new electric vehicle.

Why This Matters

Transportation is our biggest source of pollution

In 2018 Las Positas emitted 13,239 metric tons of CO2, roughly the volume of 34 Olympic swimming pools. Most of it never came from the buildings. It came from the drive to campus.

Field research

Everyone drives alone

Every person we interviewed commuted solo. One drives roughly 200 miles a day, four days a week. Switching from gas to a hybrid cut one driver's fuel from $80/day to $55/week.

Field research

Real resistance to EVs

Drivers see full EVs as too expensive and worry about range. Prices never fell as predicted. The barrier is buy-in and cost, not willingness.

Campus walk

The campus runs hot

Many uncomfortable hot spots, and the new amphitheater is bare concrete with no shade for seating. Too many ornamental crape myrtles where fast-growing native Valley Oaks would cool the campus.

Plan Breakdown

Four steps from parking lots to clean transportation

The proposal is a closed loop: build solar on the lots we already use, stop paying PG&E, and put the savings back into the commute that causes most of our emissions.

1

Build carport solar

Add roughly 2 MW of solar canopies across 11 parking lots in three construction phases, bringing total campus capacity to about 4.35 MW. Lot O leads Phase 1 because it sits next to the existing 2.35 MW array and grid tie-in.

~$6.2M gross capex · 2 yr to Permission to Operate
See site plan →
2

Finance the build

Stack federal ITC (~30%), a 1% CEC loan capped at $3M, and district tools: bonds, parcel tax, state grants, and supplemental loans. Debt service stays far below the utility bill the project replaces.

~$1.9M ITC · up to $3M CEC loan · bonds & grants fill the gap
See capital stack →
3

Offset PG&E & generate surplus

Self-use at the 43¢ retail rate is the engine, not grid export at 9¢. Avoiding the campus electricity bill frees roughly $1M/year, with parking revenue collected each fall at registration.

~$1M/yr avoided cost · cash-positive from year one
Run the financial model →
4

Reinvest in clean commuting

Annual profit funds free EV charging across the lots and a campus grant program students and staff apply to for help buying a new EV. Heavier fees on gas vehicles help pay for the transition.

Priority: charging & EV grants, then transit, shade & staff

Path to Operation

Two-year registration timeline

From board approval to Permission to Operate in roughly two years, with soft costs of about $75k to $150k total. The PG&E Rule 21 interconnection study is the critical path. Because LPC already has a 2.35 MW system connected, we may file an expedited amendment and cut 4 to 6 months off it.

~24 months board approval to PTO Rule 21 study = critical path Step 8 = go-live
The Payoff

Where the freed-up money goes

Once solar offsets the PG&E bill, the annual surplus is reinvested on campus, starting with free EV charging and a grant program that helps students and staff afford a new electric vehicle. The parking pass rises only about $5 for most people, while a heavier fee on gas vehicles helps fund the transition.

Charging

Free EV charging

Plant profits fund abundant, free charging across the carport lots, installed and operated from the annual surplus so range anxiety stops being a reason to say no to electric.

Grant program

EV purchase grants

A portion of each year's profit goes into a campus grant pool. Students and staff apply for awards that cover part of the cost of buying a new EV, lowering the upfront price that field research showed was the biggest barrier.

Transit

Better transit access

Funding to connect campus to nodes like the planned Valley Link hydrogen train near Isabel and the freeway.

Shade

Carports as shade

The panels double as cover for cars and people, cooling the hot spots students pointed out.

Jobs

Staff & CTE programs

Revenue covers power-plant staff and can open new Career & Technical Education programs in clean energy.

Resilience

Power during outages

On-site generation and storage keep critical campus systems running when the grid goes down.

Reinvestment priority: after debt service and operations, surplus profit flows first to free EV charging and the EV purchase grant program, then to transit, shade, and staff. Grant size and eligibility would be set by the district with board approval. Proven elsewhere: San Diego Community College District reports saving over $1M a year across the district, and Glendale College secured a $3M CEC loan at 1% for a 784 kW carport system in February 2025.
Capital Stack

Financing breakdown

For a ~2 MW carport build of about $6.2M gross, no single source covers the full cost. The stack combines federal tax credits, a capped 1% state loan, and district and local tools: bonds, parcel tax, state grants, and supplemental loans.

CEC 1% loan, up to $3M
Bonds, grants & parcel tax, ~$1.3M

Risk Register

Challenges and open questions

A candid accounting of what could complicate or delay the project.

Grid

PG&E grid upgrade costs

The study may find the local grid needs upgrades to handle the added export. That bill lands on us and can run from $50k to $500k or more. We will not know until the study is done.

Market

NEM 3.0 low export rates

Export pays only 8 to 10 cents per kWh, about 75% less than the old NEM 2.0. The real value is self-use at 43 cents, so storage has to absorb the summer surplus.

Structure

Seismic and wind loads

Livermore sits in a seismic zone and gets strong wind off the Altamont Pass. Every carport needs engineered footings and wind-load calculations, adding cost over rooftop solar.

Safety

Battery fire code

Adding 2,000 to 4,000 kWh of lithium storage triggers NFPA 855 review by the Livermore Fire Department, with setbacks, ventilation, and inspections.

Supply

Contractor availability

The Bay Area solar market is busy. A carport build across 11 lots could face 6 to 12 month waits for a start date, worsened by Alameda County permit backlogs.

Lifespan

Equipment replacement

Batteries last about 10 years and inverters 10 to 15. Panels keep 80% output at 25 years, with premium panels warrantied up to 40. Replacement must be budgeted.

Legal

Selling power means regulation

Generating more than we use under NEM does not make us a power plant. Selling commercially engages FERC, CPUC, and CEC. We would need an energy attorney to structure it.

Policy

Federal funding instability

The ITC and federal incentives can shift with policy, so the ~30% credit that shrinks the financed amount carries real exposure if rules change.
